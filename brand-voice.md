## Cheri's Voice — The Rules

Cheri's voice is warm, direct, and teaching-focused. She sounds like a knowledgeable friend in your kitchen, not a professor at a podium.

### Tone Principles

**Validate first, teach second.**
Before explaining what to do, acknowledge what the reader is going through.
- ✅ "No worries — I've seen this happen to thousands of students."
- ✅ "You're not alone. This is the most common question I get."
- ❌ Jumping straight into instructions without acknowledging the struggle.

**One point per paragraph, then stop.**
If the point lands in two sentences, stop at two sentences. Do not restate the same idea in different words.
- ✅ "Some people's livers convert THC efficiently; others don't — which is why the same edible can overwhelm one person and barely affect another. If edibles consistently feel too weak, even at higher doses, this is the most likely cause."
- ❌ Following that with two more sentences saying the same thing differently.

**Direct, never condescending.**
State facts plainly. When something won't work, say so and move on. Don't soften a hard truth until it loses meaning — but don't be cruel about it either.
- ✅ "You must have lab test results or product label information if you are dosing with CBD oil — it's impossible to guess the percentage of CBD in cannabis, and guessing will make your results completely unreliable."
- ❌ "You might want to consider perhaps looking into getting some lab results, if possible..."

**Short punchy sentences after longer explanatory ones.**
Use rhythm. A long sentence followed by a short declarative one creates emphasis.
- ✅ "...which is why edibles hit harder, take longer to kick in, and last significantly longer than smoked cannabis. They're a fundamentally different experience."
- ✅ Fragment sentences starting with "And" for rhythmic emphasis: "And have it taste good. And have it carry a dosage that meets your needs." — deliberate fragments, not errors.
- ✅ Enthusiastic short-sentence pivots to build energy: "But it's way more exciting than that!" — forward-leaning, not a conclusion.

**Parenthetical warmth.**
Cheri uses parenthetical asides to create intimacy.
- ✅ "(we know because our students tell us this all the time)"
- ✅ "(watch out for portion control and dosing!)"
- ✅ "(I learned this the hard way!)"

**Self-deprecation is in-voice.**
Cheri freely admits her own struggles and imperfections. This makes her relatable, not preachy — and it's distinct from false modesty.
- ✅ "I know, that's not an easy thing to do sometimes, even for me."
- ✅ "I learned this lesson the hard way."
- ✅ "I didn't publish any gummy recipes for years. Why? Because even I couldn't get them to work consistently."

**Named, specific examples over abstract placeholders.**
Cheri uses examples that feel personal and specific rather than generic.
- ✅ "How fun would it be to take Grandma Sue's apple pie recipe or Uncle Lou's BBQ sauce and turn them into edibles?"
- ❌ "Imagine turning your favorite recipes into edibles."

**Self-aware about well-worn phrases.**
When Cheri uses a cliché, she acknowledges it rather than pretending it's original. This shows self-awareness without undermining the point.
- ✅ "Cliché as it may sound, 'today is the first day of the rest of your life' has real wisdom in it."
- ❌ Using the cliché straight without acknowledging it.

**Always explain the WHY behind the WHAT.**
Never just give instructions. The reader needs to understand the reasoning so they trust the advice and remember it.

### Language Register

**Layman first, medical when useful.**
Most readers aren't medical professionals. Clinical terms ("downregulate," "endocannabinoid system," "11-hydroxy-THC") can be used to *introduce* a concept when precision matters — but define it in plain language immediately, then use the plain version for the rest of the piece.
- ✅ Introduce "CB1 receptors start to downregulate" once, explain it as "get less sensitive," then say "get less sensitive" from there on.
- ❌ Switching back and forth between clinical and plain terms for the same concept.

**Casual cannabis vocabulary is the baseline.**
"Weed," "pot," and "get high" are not bad words. Cheri uses them freely. Don't default to "consume cannabis" when "smoke some weed" fits better. Don't say "experience the effects" when "get high" is what's happening. Clinical phrasing is available when needed — but everyday vocabulary is the default.

**Warmth before technical content — in plain language.**
Never open a technical explanation cold. Frame what's coming in terms the reader already understands.
- ✅ "Here's what's actually going on in your body when you eat an edible..."
- ✅ "The reason this happens comes down to one thing your liver does..."
- ❌ "This next part gets a bit technical, but stay with me" (telegraphs a tone shift without softening it)
- ❌ Opening directly with compound names or clinical processes

The warmth beat and the plain-language rule work together: if the warmth beat itself uses clinical language, it's not warmth.

## Cannabis Terminology — Non-Negotiable

These rules apply to ALL content. No exceptions.

- **THCa → THC, CBDa → CBD.** Always use precise conversion language. Decarboxylation converts THCa and CBDa into THC and CBD.
- **Never say "activates THC."** THC doesn't get activated — THCa gets converted to THC through decarboxylation.
- **Dosing problems within a single batch** = technique/mixing issues (uneven distribution, hot spots), NOT calculation errors.
- **Dosing problems between batches** = different starting material, inconsistent decarb, or changed variables.
- **Never recommend Ardent products.** Affiliate program has deteriorated. Do not mention.

## Compliance — Mandatory for All Content

- ✅ Discuss cannabis openly and factually
- ✅ Discuss consumption methods, products, and effects educationally
- ✅ Cannabis can support pain management, sleep, stress — at a high level
- ✅ Frame benefits through health and wellness: pain management, sleep quality, anxiety relief, inflammation, general wellbeing
- ❌ **NEVER** make specific medical claims
- ❌ **NEVER** suggest cannabis cures any condition
- ❌ **NEVER** use paid advertising language (cannabis ads are restricted on Google, Meta, and all major platforms)
- ❌ Always include a medical disclaimer when discussing health benefits: *"Consult your doctor before using cannabis for medical purposes"*

### Content Framing
- Lead with: education, wellness, lifestyle, personal empowerment
- Entry point messaging: "Become an informed cannabis consumer"
- Avoid: recreational drug framing in professional/marketing content
- "Getting high" is fine in educational content about edibles effects — it's NOT fine in ad copy, email subject lines, or public-facing marketing materials

## Platform-Specific Voice Notes

**Blog posts / SEO content:** Full Cheri voice. Educational, warm, thorough. Health and wellness framing. AEO-optimized with answer-first paragraphs under each H2.

**Email sequences:** Conversational Cheri. Shorter paragraphs. More personal asides. Always includes a reason to reply ("Hit reply and let me know..."). Sign-off matches content type.

**YouTube (under 5 min):** Educational, structured, authoritative Cheri. Teases course content but never gives the full lesson for free. CTA pattern: "Get the full lesson + [feature] inside [course name]."

**TikTok / Instagram Reels (60-90 sec):** Entertainment-first, education-second. 2-second hooks. Casual, authentic Cheri — NOT the polished YouTube version. Requires separate scripts from YouTube (never repurposed cuts).

**Sales pages:** Problem → empathy → solution → proof → offer. Emotion before logic. Testimonials before curriculum. Show $89 on every CTA button (warm funnel pages).

**Social posts:** Brief, punchy, shareable. One idea per post. Link to full content.
