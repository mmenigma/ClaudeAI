**Q: How long do edibles take to kick in?**
Edibles typically take 30 minutes to 2 hours to take effect, depending on your metabolism, whether you've eaten recently, and the type of edible. Oils and tinctures absorb faster than solid foods. This delayed onset is why people accidentally eat too much — they don't feel anything after 45 minutes and take more. Always wait the full 2 hours before deciding a dose didn't work.

**Q: Why don't edibles work for me even though I smoke regularly?**
Your tolerance to smoked cannabis doesn't automatically transfer to edibles. Edibles are processed through your liver, which converts THC into a more potent compound (11-hydroxy-THC). Some people's livers process this differently, meaning the same edible can hit one person hard and barely affect another. If edibles consistently don't work for you, try taking them with a fatty meal, which improves absorption significantly.

**Q: How long do edibles last compared to smoking?**
Edible effects typically last 4 to 8 hours — sometimes longer at higher doses — compared to 1 to 3 hours for smoked cannabis. This longer duration is one reason many medical users prefer edibles, especially for sleep or sustained pain relief.

**Q: What's the right starting dose for someone new to edibles?**
If you've never had edibles before, start with 5mg THC or less. Wait the full 2 hours before deciding whether it's working. If you feel nothing, try 5–10mg next time. Going slowly avoids the most common mistake beginners make: eating a second dose too soon and ending up far more medicated than intended.

**Q: Can you make edibles with any cannabis strain?**
Yes — any cannabis flower, concentrate, or infusion can be used in edibles. The strain will affect the flavor of your infused butter or oil slightly, but the cooking process mostly neutralizes strain-specific taste differences. What matters more for edibles is the THC and CBD percentages in your starting material, since those determine the final dose.

**Q: What's the difference between cannabis-infused butter and cannabis oil for edibles?**
Both work for most recipes. Cannabutter works better in baked goods where butter is traditional (cookies, brownies). Cannabis-infused coconut oil or olive oil is more versatile for savory cooking and works well anywhere you'd use oil. The cannabinoids bind to fat in either case, so the infusion process and potency math is essentially the same.

**Q: How do you calculate milligrams of THC in homemade edibles?**
The basic formula is: (grams of cannabis × THC% as a decimal × 1000) ÷ number of servings = mg THC per serving. For example, 7 grams of 15% THC cannabis contains about 1,050mg THC total. If your recipe makes 24 brownies, each brownie has roughly 43mg — higher than most beginners want. This is why knowing your starting potency and planning your batch size together is so important.

**Q: What if I don't know the THC percentage of my cannabis?**
If your cannabis is unlabeled, you can make a reasonable estimate. Typical street cannabis ranges from 10–20% THC. Using 15% as a conservative estimate is a safe starting point. If you bought from a dispensary, the percentage should be on the label. Our Cannabis Dosing Mastery course covers how to estimate potency in unlabeled cannabis more accurately.

**Q: Why are my homemade edibles inconsistent batch to batch?**
Inconsistency usually comes from one of three sources: different cannabis potency between batches, inconsistent decarboxylation (which converts THCa to active THC), or uneven mixing of your infused butter or oil into the recipe. Even distribution is a particular problem with fats — if your cannabutter isn't thoroughly mixed into the batter before baking, some pieces will be stronger than others.

**Q: How do I make edibles that are a consistent 10mg per serving?**
Start by calculating the total THC in your batch using the formula above, then work backward to determine how much cannabutter or oil to use to hit your target per-serving dose. **Q: Why do edibles work for some people but not others?**
Individual differences in metabolism and liver enzyme activity are the main reason. Edibles are processed through your liver, which converts THC into 11-hydroxy-THC — a compound that's actually more potent and longer-lasting than inhaled THC. Some people's livers produce this conversion efficiently; others don't. This is genetic and there's no way to "train" it. If edibles consistently don't affect you even at high doses, this liver enzyme variation may be the reason.

**Q: Does eating food before taking edibles make them work better or worse?**
Eating a meal with healthy fats (avocado, nuts, olive oil, full-fat dairy) before or alongside edibles can significantly improve absorption — some studies show up to 4x better absorption with a fatty meal. An empty stomach can sometimes make onset faster but less consistent. The worst scenario is taking edibles right after a heavy meal of processed foods, which may slow absorption unpredictably.

**Q: How long should I wait before deciding my edible dose didn't work?**
Always wait at least 2 hours, preferably 2.5. The most common mistake is taking a second dose at the 45-minute or 1-hour mark when nothing's happened yet — only to have both doses hit at once an hour later. Factors like meal timing, individual metabolism, and the specific edible type all affect onset timing. When in doubt, wait it out.

**Q: Can I increase how well edibles work by changing what I eat with them?**
Yes. Taking edibles alongside foods high in healthy fats — avocado, full-fat yogurt, nuts, coconut oil — improves THC absorption because cannabinoids are fat-soluble. Some people also report that medium-chain triglycerides (MCT oil) are particularly effective. This is why cannabis-infused coconut oil is so popular — the MCT content may improve bioavailability on its own.

**Q: Does building tolerance to smoking affect how edibles work?**
Somewhat, but the relationship isn't direct. Edibles produce a different metabolite (11-hydroxy-THC) than inhaled cannabis, so your smoking tolerance doesn't fully transfer. Frequent edible users can build edible-specific tolerance. Taking a 2–3 day tolerance break often restores edible sensitivity significantly.

**FAQ block to add:**

**Q: How does cannabis edibles tolerance work?**
When you consume edibles regularly, your endocannabinoid receptors (primarily CB1 receptors) gradually downregulate — meaning they become less responsive to THC. This happens with both smoked and edible cannabis, but the metabolite produced by edibles (11-hydroxy-THC) creates a somewhat different tolerance pattern than inhaled THC. The result is that you need more over time to feel the same effect.

**Q: Does smoking tolerance transfer to edibles?**
Partially, but not completely. Because edibles produce a different primary compound than smoked cannabis, you can have a high smoking tolerance and still be significantly affected by edibles — and vice versa. Many experienced smokers are surprised by how strongly edibles hit them even after years of regular cannabis use.

**Q: How long does a tolerance break take to work for edibles?**
Most people notice meaningful sensitivity improvement after just 2–3 days without cannabis. A full 2-week break typically restores close to baseline sensitivity. Even a single day off can have a noticeable effect. The key is consistency — breaking for 3 days every week has more cumulative impact than taking one big break every few months.

**Q: What's the fastest way to reset edibles tolerance?**
A complete cannabinoid abstinence break is the only reliable method — no flower, no edibles, no concentrates. Some people find that CBD alone (without THC) during a tolerance break doesn't interfere with the reset process, but the evidence is anecdotal. Exercise and good sleep support the endocannabinoid system generally, which may help sensitivity return faster during a break.

**Q: Can you microdose edibles to avoid building tolerance?**
Yes — microdosing (typically 2.5–5mg THC) is often cited as a way to get therapeutic benefits while minimizing tolerance buildup. The theory is that lower doses may not downregulate CB1 receptors as aggressively as full doses. Many medical users prefer microdosing for exactly this reason, combining low-dose edibles with occasional higher-dose use when needed.

**FAQ block to add:**

**Q: Why are my homemade edibles so weak even when I use a lot of cannabis?**
The most common culprit is skipping or rushing decarboxylation. Raw cannabis contains THCa, which is non-intoxicating. Heat converts it to THC during decarboxylation. If your cannabis wasn't properly decarbed before infusion, you may have lost 50% or more of your potential potency before you even started cooking. Other causes: too-high cooking temperature during infusion, which destroys THC, or not enough fat in the recipe to carry the cannabinoids.

**Q: Does cooking temperature destroy THC in edibles?**
Yes — excessive heat degrades THC. THC begins to break down above about 245°F (118°C). However, the internal temperature of most baked goods stays well below this, even when your oven is set to 350°F or higher. The edible itself doesn't reach oven temperature. The danger zone is extended cooking at high heat, or overheating your infused butter/oil during the infusion process.

**Q: How long should you decarboxylate cannabis for edibles?**
The most common recommendation is 240–245°F for 40–60 minutes in a conventional oven. Times vary based on your oven's accuracy, moisture content of your cannabis, and desired activation level. Some people use lower temperatures (220°F) for longer times (90 minutes) to preserve more terpenes. An oven thermometer is worth it — most home ovens run 15–25°F off from their dial setting.

**Q: How do you prevent uneven dosing in homemade edibles?**
Thorough mixing is critical. Your infused butter or oil must be completely homogenized throughout the batter or dough before baking. For brownies and bar cookies, also mix the batter extensively after adding the fat. For cookies, let chilled dough come to room temperature for more even consistency. Cutting your finished product into uniform pieces also makes a big difference in portion consistency.

**Q: Is it more efficient to use less cannabis and infuse a smaller amount of butter?**
Generally yes — a higher cannabis-to-butter ratio typically leads to better extraction efficiency than a very dilute infusion. Using 1 oz of cannabis per 1 cup of butter extracts more efficiently than using the same ounce in 4 cups of butter, because the infusion saturation point affects how much THC actually binds. However, you'll need less infused butter per recipe, so calculate accordingly.

**Q: Can you really add cannabis to any recipe?**
Almost any recipe can be made into an edible by substituting cannabutter for regular butter, or cannabis-infused oil for regular oil. **Q: How do you substitute cannabutter in a regular recipe?**

Rice Krispie treats are the classic beginner choice — no baking required, just melt cannabutter with marshmallows and cereal. If you can melt butter, you can make them. **Q: Do cannabis topicals get you high?**
No — standard cannabis topicals (creams, salves, balms) don't produce any intoxicating effects. When applied to skin, the cannabinoids absorb locally but don't enter the bloodstream at levels that affect the brain. The one exception is transdermal cannabis patches, which are specifically designed for systemic absorption and can produce noticeable effects. For everyday topical use, there's no high.

**Q: What conditions are cannabis topicals most commonly used for?**
Cannabis topicals are most commonly used for localized pain (joint pain, muscle soreness, arthritis), skin conditions (psoriasis, eczema), inflammation, and post-workout recovery. Many users also report benefits for minor burns, dry skin, and tension headaches when applied to temples. Effects are localized to the area of application.

**Q: Who should avoid cannabis topicals?**
People with known sensitivity to cannabis or any carrier ingredient in the specific product (coconut oil, shea butter, essential oils) should patch test first. Broken skin or open wounds should generally be avoided. People subject to drug testing should be cautious — while most standard topicals don't cause failed drug tests, transdermal patches that deliver cannabinoids into the bloodstream might. Anyone on blood thinners or other medications should check with a pharmacist.

**Q: Can you make cannabis topicals at home?**
Yes — homemade cannabis topicals are simpler to make than edibles and don't require precise dosing since they're not ingested. A basic cannabis salve requires only infused coconut oil, beeswax, and any additional essential oils you choose.
